Me at 27

I've always been someone who thinks a lot about why things are the way they are. Not in a loud or academic way, just naturally. If something happens, I usually don't just take it at face value. I want to understand what's underneath it, and whether it could be different.

I think a lot of that comes from how I grew up.

I'm an only child, and people joke about that, but it actually shaped how I interact with people more than I realized at the time. If you have siblings, there's a built-in system. You can argue, you can be annoying, you can push things a little too far, and somehow you still end up interacting. There's always a path back in.

I didn't really have that. If I wanted people to engage with me, I had to give them a reason to. It either worked or it didn't. There wasn't a fallback.

So I learned early that if I wanted connection, I had to be someone people wanted to connect with. Not in a fake way, just in a way where I was paying attention to them.

My parents also had me later, so they were older, and they were big on respect. Not just in a strict way, but in a "be aware of how you show up" kind of way. Tone mattered. The way you said something mattered just as much as what you said.

So over time, I think I just became very aware of people. What they were saying, how they were saying it, what they cared about, what made them open up or shut down.

And once you start paying attention like that, you begin to notice patterns.

One of the biggest ones is that people don't actually feel seen as often as you'd think. Even people who are around others all the time.

If you give someone space to talk about something they genuinely care about, and you're actually listening, not just waiting for your turn to speak, something shifts. They relax a little. They start saying things they weren't planning to say.

It's a small thing, but it has a big effect.

And it only works if it's real. People can tell when you're doing something because you read it somewhere versus when you actually understand why it matters.

It's kind of like humor. The Office works because it understands what's normal and then pushes just slightly outside of it. Not too far, just enough. When something goes too far, it stops feeling funny and just feels off.

Same thing with people. There's a line between what feels natural and what feels forced, and most people can feel it even if they can't explain it.

So a lot of how I interact with people is just paying attention to that line. What feels normal here? What feels slightly different in a good way? What would feel fake?

That way of thinking carries over into how I approach problems too.

When I'm trying to understand something, I usually break it down pretty simply. What's happening right now? What do we actually want instead? What's in the way? What's the gap between those two?

I use simple analogies in my head for this. Food is an easy one.

What are you eating right now? What do you want? To be healthy, to be in shape? What's stopping you? Time, money, habits?

But that's just the surface level.

The part I care more about is what happens if you actually get what you say you want.

If someone says they want to be in great shape, that sounds straightforward. But if they woke up tomorrow with that outcome, their entire life would have to adjust to maintain it. New routines, new habits, even how they see themselves.

Same thing in work. People say they want better systems, better data, better tools. But those things come with changes. Ownership changes, workflows change, expectations change.

So I find myself asking, even if I don't say it directly, are you actually ready for that version of things? Not just do you want it, but can you operate in it?

Because getting what you want and being ready for it are two different things.

At the same time, not every situation needs that level of thinking.

If someone hasn't eaten in three days, the answer isn't to explore their long-term goals. They need food. That's it. The severity of the situation kind of tells you how deep to go.

But when it's something bigger, something layered, something that's going to affect how people work or how decisions get made, that's when it makes sense to go deeper. That's when you start asking second, third, fourth-level questions. Not to make things complicated, but to understand what's actually driving what's happening.

And I think that's part of why people say I'm good at discovery or that I ask good questions.

I don't feel like I'm doing anything special. I'm mostly just trying to understand what's actually going on instead of stopping at the first answer.

That shows up in small ways too.

I try to give people space to talk. I pay attention to details. I remember things people mention. Sometimes it's as simple as bringing coffee or cookies to a meeting. Not because I expect anything from it, but because it shows I'm paying attention in a way that isn't obvious.

Those small things add up.

Because at the end of the day, trust isn't just someone liking you. It's someone feeling comfortable enough to put their name next to yours.

If someone introduces you to their manager, they're taking a risk. They're saying, "I trust this person enough to bring them into a bigger conversation." And they only do that if they feel like you understand them and won't make them look bad in that next interaction.

That's a big part of how I think about my role too.

I work in a technical space, but I don't think the technical part is the most important part. It has to be right, but it's not the starting point.

People have to want the solution before the solution matters.

You can build the best tool in the world, but if nobody wants it, it doesn't get used. It's like cooking the best steak possible and serving it in a room full of vegans. It might be objectively great, but it's the wrong thing for that room.

So a lot of what I focus on is alignment. Do we actually want the same thing? Does this make sense for you? Are you ready for what comes with it?

Once that's there, the technical side becomes a lot easier to navigate.

I'm still figuring parts of this out though.

I naturally adapt to people. I read the room, adjust how I speak, how I approach things. That's been helpful, but there's a point where you can do that too much and lose your own voice in the process.

I also don't really like compliments. I tend to downplay things or frame them as "it seems like" instead of just accepting them. I'm aware of it, I just haven't fully worked through it yet.

And even with writing this, I think a lot about voice.

I don't want to sound like everyone else. I don't want something that feels polished but doesn't actually sound like me. I'd rather it feel a little imperfect and real, like you're actually hearing how I think instead of reading something that was cleaned up too much.

This is probably the closest way I can describe how I see things right now.

It'll change over time, but this is where I'm at.
